\begin{abstract}
\textbf{Objective:}
Cardiopulmonary exercise testing (CPET) is the gold standard for exercise safety
assessment, yet data-driven safety zone quantification for Chinese elderly
hypertensive patients has not been established. This study aimed to construct a
data-driven exercise safety zone based on the R/T/I (Reserve/Threshold/Instability)
three-axis framework, develop an improved reference equation adapted to this
population, and quantify systematic deviations between data-driven thresholds
and published reference values.

\textbf{Methods:}
Retrospective single-centre study (N=3,206, $\geq$60 years) using a hypertension
history (HTN) $\times$ exercise-induced hypoxaemia (EIH) 2$\times$2 design with four
cohorts. The R/T/I axes (cardiopulmonary reserve, ventilatory threshold stability,
haemodynamic instability) were combined with equal weighting into a continuous
$S_{\mathrm{lab}}$ score; safety zone thresholds were determined by reference
population percentiles (P75/P90). An improved reference equation incorporated BMI
in addition to traditional age/sex variables, validated by 5-fold cross-validation.
Bootstrap resampling (1000 iterations) estimated 95\% confidence intervals for
thresholds, which were systematically compared against Weber-Janicki
classification criteria.

\textbf{Results:}
The improved reference equation raised VO$_2$peak $R^2$ from 0.298 to 0.392
($\Delta R^2$=+0.094); existing external reference equations yielded $R^2<0$ in this
population. Bootstrap CI widths for both thresholds were $<$1.0 (Green/Yellow: 0.65;
Yellow/Red: 0.94), indicating highly stable thresholds. The Yellow/Red threshold
deviated from Weber-Janicki criteria by +21\% (VO$_2$peak\%pred 71\% vs 50\%),
while the Green/Yellow threshold deviated by only +5\%. Reclassification rate was
39.5\%, including 188 Red$\leftrightarrow$Green crossover cases.

\textbf{Conclusions:}
This study presents the first data-driven R/T/I three-axis CPET safety zone
framework, with Bootstrap-validated robust thresholds (CI width $<$1.0 for both
boundaries). The published Yellow/Red threshold shows a +21\% systematic
deviation in this elderly hypertensive population, necessitating population-specific
calibration. The BMI-enhanced reference equation substantially outperforms existing
external equations ($\Delta R^2$=+0.094). This framework provides a reproducible,
data-driven paradigm for personalised CPET safety stratification in Chinese elderly
hypertensive patients.

\textbf{Keywords:} cardiopulmonary exercise testing; data-driven safety zone;
hypertension; exercise-induced hypoxaemia; R/T/I three-axis framework;
reference equation; threshold calibration
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
% ============================================================

Against a backdrop of global ageing, cardiovascular disease (CVD) has become the
leading cause of disability and death among older adults~\cite{WHO2023}. Exercise
rehabilitation is well established to improve cardiopulmonary function and long-term
prognosis in CVD patients~\cite{Piepoli2016,Thomas2019}; however, the risk of
exercise-induced adverse events—particularly exercise-induced hypoxaemia (EIH) and
hypertensive stress responses—substantially constrains the precise implementation of
exercise prescriptions, underscoring the need for objective tools to define the
exercise safety boundary in elderly cardiovascular patients~\cite{Myers2013,Agostoni2013}.

Cardiopulmonary exercise testing provides comprehensive cardiopulmonary assessment
and represents the gold standard for exercise safety prescription~\cite{Balady2010,Guazzi2012}.
Existing safety stratification systems, notably the Weber-Janicki functional
classification~\cite{Weber1982}, were derived from Western healthy populations or
specific disease cohorts and have not been empirically validated in Chinese elderly
hypertensive patients through large-scale studies. The Chinese expert consensus on
CPET~\cite{SunXG2022consensus} recommends standardised CPET protocols, yet
quantitative safety zone frameworks with locally calibrated thresholds remain absent
for this population.

Three methodological gaps underlie this limitation.
(a) The interaction between HTN history and EIH has not been systematically quantified
in large samples, leaving unclear whether these factors operate through independent
multi-axis mechanisms that would support a multi-dimensional framework design.
(b) Existing reference equations—restricted to age and sex~\cite{Wasserman1999,Hansen1984,Koch2009}—exhibit
limited precision in elderly hypertensive patients with pronounced differences in body
composition, as BMI has not been incorporated.
(c) A data-driven CPET safety zone threshold calibration methodology has not been
established; a systematic framework to integrate multidimensional CPET information
into robust, quantitative stratification is lacking~\cite{Ross2024}.

This study was designed to address these gaps using CPET data from 3,206 elderly
hypertensive patients at a single centre. We propose the R/T/I three-axis
data-driven safety zone framework, integrating cardiopulmonary reserve (Reserve,
R-axis), ventilatory threshold stability (Threshold, T-axis), and haemodynamic
instability (Instability, I-axis) into a continuous composite score. We also develop
a BMI-enhanced reference equation, formally validate threshold robustness using
Bootstrap, and quantify the clinical necessity of population-specific calibration
through systematic comparison with Weber-Janicki reference criteria.

was approved by the institutional ethics committee (approval number:

\subsection{CPET Protocol and Variable Definitions}

CPET was performed using incremental-load cycle ergometer or treadmill protocols
with standard gas metabolic analysis. Key measures included: peak oxygen uptake
(VO$_2$peak, mL/kg/min), peak heart rate (HR$_{\text{peak}}$, bpm), oxygen pulse
(O$_2$ pulse, mL/beat), VO$_2$ at the first ventilatory threshold (VT1-VO$_2$),
$V_E/V_{\text{CO}_2}$ slope, heart rate recovery (HRR, 1 minute), oxygen uptake
efficiency slope (OUES), and peak METs. EIH was defined as a $\geq$4\% decrease in
SpO$_2$ from rest or an absolute value $<$90\% during exercise (identified by
group-coding field).

\subsection{Data Quality Control}

The quality control pipeline comprised five layers: physiological range compliance
(with clip corrections for 35 outliers), cross-field logical consistency, missingness
threshold inspection ($>$30\% flagging), protocol consistency (cycle/treadmill
standardisation), and EIH-group-specific diagnostics. Twenty-six records (0.8\%)
were excluded, yielding 3,206 for analysis.

\subsection{Improved Reference Equations}

Traditional reference equations rely solely on age and sex~\cite{Wasserman1999,Hansen1984}.
Using the reference subset (CTRL group, n=962), we compared four formula
complexities (adding BMI as predictor) and selected the optimal formulation based on
5-fold cross-validation $R^2$ and AIC. The improved reference equation (v2) for
VO$_2$peak is:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:v2}
\widehat{\text{VO}_2\text{peak}} = \alpha + \beta_1 \cdot \text{age} + \beta_2 \cdot \mathbf{1}_M + \beta_3 \cdot (\text{age} \times \mathbf{1}_M) + \beta_4 \cdot \text{BMI}
\end{equation}

Applicability of external reference equations (Wasserman \& Hansen 1999,
Koch et al. 2009~\cite{Koch2009}) was evaluated with $R^2$, RMSE, and systematic
bias to formally test the necessity of population-specific calibration.

\subsection{R/T/I Three-Axis Safety Zone Framework}

\subsubsection{Axis Score Definitions}

The safety zone framework comprises three independently scored axes:

\textbf{R-axis (Reserve, cardiopulmonary reserve):}
Equal-weight average of VO$_2$peak \%pred\_v2 (improved reference equation),
O$_2$ pulse peak (\%pred), and peak METs; inverted to a risk score
(higher = greater risk).

\textbf{T-axis (Threshold, ventilatory threshold stability):}
Percentile scores for VT1/VO$_2$peak ratio and $V_E/V_{\text{CO}_2}$ slope,
capturing aerobic metabolic stability and ventilatory efficiency.

\textbf{I-axis (Instability, haemodynamic instability):}
Percentile scores for peak systolic blood pressure and O$_2$ pulse trajectory
(peak-slope proxy), augmented by an EIH status penalty term.

\subsubsection{Composite Score and Threshold Determination}

The three axes are combined with equal weighting ($\approx$1/3 each) into a
continuous $S_{\mathrm{lab}}$ score (0--100, higher = greater risk):

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:slab}
S_\text{lab} = \frac{1}{3}(s_R + s_T + s_I)
\end{equation}

Equal weighting ensures score computation is independent of any external clinical
outcome, eliminating circular dependency. Thresholds are set by reference population
percentiles: the 75th (P75) and 90th (P90) percentiles of $S_{\mathrm{lab}}$ in the
reference subset (CTRL, n=962) define the Green/Yellow and Yellow/Red boundaries,
respectively.

\subsubsection{Threshold Robustness Validation}

Three complementary approaches validated threshold robustness.
\textbf{Bootstrap confidence intervals:} 1000 bootstrap resamples (with replacement)
estimated 95\% CIs for both thresholds; CI width $<$2 indicates high stability.
\textbf{Sensitivity scanning:} Both thresholds were offset by $\pm$5\% and
$\pm$10\%, and zone redistribution was recorded (reclassification rate $<$10\%
considered robust).
\textbf{Literature threshold comparison:} Data-driven VO$_2$peak\%pred at each
threshold was compared against Weber-Janicki criteria~\cite{Weber1982}
(Green $\geq$70\%, Yellow 50--70\%, Red $<$50\%); deviation $>$10\% indicates
necessity of population-specific calibration. A reclassification matrix against
the fixed-threshold zone was computed, with emphasis on
Red$\leftrightarrow$Green crossover cases.

\subsection{Statistical Analysis}

Baseline characteristics were compared with Kruskal-Wallis tests (continuous
variables; Shapiro-Wilk for distribution assessment) and $\chi^2$ tests (categorical
variables), with Dunn's post-hoc pairwise comparisons (Bonferroni correction,
$\alpha$=0.0083). HTN$\times$EIH interactions were assessed by Type II ANOVA
(statsmodels); effect sizes were quantified as partial $\eta^2$~\cite{Cohen1988}
(small: 0.01--0.06; medium: 0.06--0.14; large: $>$0.14). Given N=3,206, effect
size rather than $p$-value was the primary interpretive metric. As auxiliary
validation, machine learning models (LASSO/XGBoost/LightGBM/CatBoost) were used
to evaluate the predictive performance ceiling of summary-level CPET data, with a
data leakage prevention mechanism to ensure valid benchmarking; see Supplementary
Materials for details.

HTN history was the primary driver of reduced VO$_2$peak ($\eta^2$=0.084, medium effect)
and peak HR ($\eta^2$=0.133, medium effect), reflecting systemic suppression of exercise

all statistically significant interactions in this large sample had negligible effect
sizes. These findings indicate that HTN and EIH operate through the cardiopulmonary
reserve axis (R-axis) and the haemodynamic instability axis (I-axis) independently,
providing the physiological basis for separate multi-axis evaluation.

\subsection{Auxiliary Validation}

As methodological auxiliary validation, machine learning models (pre-exercise risk
prediction AUC=0.583; post-exercise safety classification F1=0.499) performed
comparably to a 2-variable clinical baseline (HTN history + BMI, AUC=0.611),
failing to exceed traditional clinical features—reflecting the predictive performance
ceiling of summary-level CPET data alone. Proxy leakage ablation analysis confirmed
negligible leakage contribution from VO$_2$peak ($\Delta$F1=+0.003), indicating
that the performance ceiling reflects intrinsic feature set limitations rather than
methodological flaws. Multi-definition concordance analysis (K=4 definitions)
identified 26.2\% high-confidence classifications (841 patients); 73.5\% showed
cross-definition discordance, reflecting structural uncertainty at the summary data
level. High-confidence Red zone event rate (28.6\%) was substantially higher than
Green zone (5.1\%), validating reliable clinical discrimination in high-confidence
classifications. Detailed model results and supplementary methods are provided in
Supplementary Materials.

\section{Discussion}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Core Findings and Clinical Implications}

This study systematically established a data-driven R/T/I three-axis exercise safety
zone framework in a cohort of 3,206 elderly hypertensive patients. The core
methodological contribution lies in integrating multidimensional cardiopulmonary
information—reserve (R-axis), ventilatory threshold stability (T-axis), and
haemodynamic instability (I-axis)—into a continuous composite score anchored to
reference population distribution boundaries. The fundamental advantage of equal
weighting is that threshold computation is entirely independent of any external
clinical outcome, avoiding circular dependency; robustness is formally quantified
by Bootstrap CI (Green/Yellow 0.65, Yellow/Red 0.94, both $<$1.0)—a metric rarely
reported in existing CPET safety zone literature. This framework extends beyond
the limitation of single-metric (VO$_2$peak\%pred) safety zone definitions~\cite{Weber1982},
providing a reproducible paradigm for systematic integration of multidimensional
CPET information.

The +21\% systematic deviation of the published Yellow/Red threshold is the most
clinically significant finding of this study. This deviation implies that directly
applying Weber-Janicki criteria to Chinese elderly hypertensive patients would
overestimate risk for approximately 21\% of patients with VO$_2$peak\%pred 50--70\%,
misclassifying them as Yellow when they belong to Green in this population.
The reclassification rate of 39.5\% and 188 Red$\leftrightarrow$Green crossover cases
quantify the clinical consequences of this threshold mismatch. The contrasting
finding—that the Green/Yellow boundary shows only a +5\% deviation—suggests that
the low-risk identification boundary transfers reasonably well across populations,
while the moderate-to-high risk boundary is the critical calibration target.
This asymmetric deviation pattern closely aligns with the call for localised
threshold validation in the Chinese expert consensus~\cite{SunXG2022consensus},
providing empirical evidence for multi-centre prospective calibration studies.

The improved reference equation incorporating BMI ($\Delta R^2$=+0.094) is
theoretically well supported: BMI serves as a readily available body composition
proxy, and both reduced muscle mass and increased fat fraction—prevalent in elderly
hypertensive patients—are independently associated with diminished cardiopulmonary
reserve. The BMI coefficient of $-$0.606 mL/kg/min per unit is consistent with
published associations between obesity and reduced exercise capacity in older adults.
The failure of external equations ($R^2<0$, maximum systematic error
$-$11.85 mL/kg/min) further confirms that even equations derived from cohorts
including Asian sub-samples (Koch et al. 2009) cannot be directly transferred to
Chinese elderly hypertensive patients, establishing the necessity of a local reference
subset (CTRL, n=962) as the foundation for reliable R-axis assessment.

The finding that HTN and EIH exert independent main effects on distinct
cardiopulmonary dimensions provides the physiological rationale for multi-axis
framework design. HTN's medium-effect impacts on exercise capacity ($\eta^2$=0.084)
and chronotropic function ($\eta^2$=0.133) reflect systematic suppression of cardiac
output reserve and autonomic regulation by hypertension~\cite{Myers2013}; EIH's
large-effect elevation of peak SBP ($\eta^2$=0.371) reveals a distinct
haemodynamic stress mechanism characteristic of EIH patients. The non-significant
HTN$\times$EIH interaction for VO$_2$peak ($p$=0.894) indicates that the two factors
exert independent additive rather than synergistic effects on cardiopulmonary reserve,
statistically supporting the principle of evaluating R and I axes separately and
independently.

\subsection{Comparison with Prior Literature}

At the methodological level, this study complements the CPET modelling data leakage
prevention framework proposed by Ross et al.~\cite{Ross2024} and the breath-by-breath
VT detection research of Zignoli et al.~\cite{Zignoli2022ejss}: the latter achieves
fine-grained stratification from raw waveform data, while this study establishes a
robust safety zone threshold methodology from summary-level data, representing
complementary CPET safety assessment approaches at different data granularities.

For reference equations, Koch et al.~\cite{Koch2009} SHIP-derived values (including
a Chinese sub-sample) yielded $R^2 \approx -0.16$ (male) and $-0.02$ (female) in
this population, confirming that even equations from partly Asian reference populations
cannot be applied to Chinese elderly hypertensive patients and reinforcing the
necessity of locally derived reference subsets. Compared with the Chinese expert
consensus~\cite{SunXG2022consensus}, this study provides the first large-sample
empirical calibration of the recommended VO$_2$peak\%pred thresholds in an elderly
hypertensive cohort.

Machine learning performance (AUC$\approx$0.58, F1$\approx$0.50) was lower than that
of Li et al.~\cite{Li2022xgb} (AUC=0.794) and Arina et al.~\cite{Arina2025}
(AUC=0.946). This performance gap reflects systematic feature set differences—our
study lacked medication data, left ventricular ejection fraction, and other key
clinical variables—rather than methodological deficiencies. This discrepancy in
fact substantiates positioning the R/T/I safety zone framework (not an ML prediction
tool) as the core contribution: the performance ceiling of summary-level CPET data
alone is insufficient for clinical prediction tasks, yet precisely adequate for
establishing stable, data-anchored stratification thresholds.

\subsection{Limitations}

The principal limitations of this study are as follows: (1) single-centre
retrospective design with potential selection bias and absence of an independent
external validation cohort; (2) complete absence of medication data—$\beta$-blocker
use significantly attenuates HR responses and may confound HR-related metrics;
(3) EIH classification derived from group-coding fields rather than continuous
real-time SpO$_2$ monitoring, introducing potential misclassification; (4) exclusive
use of summary-level CPET data without breath-by-breath waveforms, constraining
accurate VT/RCP detection and the predictive performance ceiling; (5) equal weighting
of R/T/I axes—the relative contribution of each axis requires prospective validation;
(6) reference equation $R^2$=0.392 retains substantial residual variance, and
the oldest subgroup ($\geq$75 years) warrants further validation.

\subsection{Future Directions}

Future research should incorporate breath-by-breath gas exchange and 12-lead ECG
waveform data to overcome the predictive ceiling of summary-level data and to
enable accurate VT1/RCP detection at the waveform level, providing more reliable
T-axis inputs~\cite{Hearn2018,Zignoli2022ejss}. Multi-centre prospective validation
is the critical next step for generalising this framework and should incorporate
complete medication data (particularly $\beta$-blockers) to correct related biases,
and independently validate age- and sex-stratified thresholds. The ability of
wearable devices to proxy key CPET anchor variables (VO$_2$peak, HR$_{\text{peak}}$,
peak SBP) warrants systematic exploration~\cite{Gao2026}, to evaluate the feasibility
of home exercise safety monitoring.

% ============================================================
\section{Conclusions}
% ============================================================

This study establishes the first data-driven R/T/I three-axis exercise safety zone
framework, with reference population percentile-based thresholds validated by
Bootstrap as highly robust (CI width $<$1.0 for both boundaries), providing a
reproducible data-driven paradigm for CPET safety zone quantification in Chinese
elderly hypertensive patients. The published Yellow/Red threshold shows a +21\%
systematic deviation in this population (Green/Yellow only +5\%), necessitating
population-specific calibration for moderate-to-high risk stratification; a
reclassification rate of 39.5\% quantifies the clinical cost of direct application
of literature-derived thresholds. The BMI-enhanced improved reference equation
substantially outperforms traditional equations ($\Delta R^2$=+0.094), and existing
external reference equations ($R^2<0$) cannot be applied directly to this population.
Future studies incorporating breath-by-breath waveform data and multi-centre
prospective validation are needed to further enhance the clinical utility and
generalisability of this framework.
